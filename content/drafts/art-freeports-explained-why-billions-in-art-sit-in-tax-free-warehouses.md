# Art Freeports Explained: Why Billions in Art Sit in Tax-Free Warehouses

Art freeports are high-security warehouses where collectors and investors can store, buy, sell, and even restore artwork without paying import duties, VAT, or sales tax, for as long as the work stays inside. The Geneva Freeport alone holds an estimated $100 billion in fine art and other goods, with roughly 40% of its total stock consisting of art, including an estimated 1,000 Picassos. For investors, freeports matter because they affect how art moves through the global market, where it sits, who can see it, and how it gets priced. They also carry growing regulatory risk that could change the rules for anyone using them.

This article explains how freeports work, why wealthy collectors use them, which ones matter most, what regulatory shifts are underway, and what all of this means for art as an investment.

## How Freeports Work

A freeport is a customs-approved zone where goods are treated as "in transit" rather than imported into the host country. This legal fiction is the core of the whole system. Because the art never formally enters the country, no import duties or value-added taxes apply. The tax clock only starts if and when the work leaves the freeport and clears customs into a taxable territory.

Inside a freeport, collectors can do almost everything they would do with art outside one. They can store it in climate-controlled vaults with armed security. They can have it restored or photographed. They can show it to prospective buyers in private viewing rooms. They can sell it to another collector, who takes ownership inside the same facility. And they can borrow against it, using the stored art as collateral for loans, without ever moving it.

The key point for investors: art can change hands multiple times inside a freeport without triggering any tax event. A collector can buy a painting for $5 million, sell it for $8 million to another buyer in the same vault, and pay no capital gains tax on the $3 million profit, because the transaction took place in a zone where no country's tax rules apply.

## Why Collectors Use Them

The financial logic is straightforward, and the savings can be large.

**VAT and import duty deferral.** In most European countries, importing a work of art triggers VAT of 5% to 20%, depending on the country. A French collector buying a $10 million painting at auction in New York would owe French VAT on import. By shipping the work directly to a Geneva or Luxembourg freeport instead, that bill is deferred for as long as the art stays inside. On a $10 million work, the savings can run to $500,000 or more.

**Capital gains deferral.** Because sales inside a freeport happen outside any tax jurisdiction, gains on those sales are not taxed at the point of transaction. Collectors who buy and sell within the freeport can compound returns without giving up a share to capital gains taxes at each step.

**Privacy.** Freeports offer a level of discretion that open-market transactions do not. Sales are private, ownership can be held through corporate structures, and there is no public auction record. For collectors who value secrecy, whether for security, estate planning, or other reasons, this is a strong draw.

**Practical convenience.** The best freeports offer museum-grade storage, insurance, restoration, and logistics under one roof. For collectors with large holdings spread across countries, a freeport can serve as a central hub for managing a collection.

Since 2022, freeport operators have reported a meaningful rise in clients, with collectors moving holdings into freeport storage in response to geopolitical instability, including the Ukraine war and shifting trade policy. The Art Newspaper reported in 2025 that "anxious collectors are increasingly turning to freeport havens."

## The Major Freeports

Not all freeports are equal. A handful of facilities handle the vast bulk of the world's stored art.

**Geneva Freeport, Switzerland.** The oldest and largest, with an estimated $100 billion in total holdings. Art makes up roughly 40% of that, alongside wine, precious metals, and luxury goods. The facility has been in operation since 1888 and remains the default choice for European and international collectors. Switzerland charges 7.7% VAT on art that leaves the freeport and enters Swiss territory.

**Luxembourg Freeport.** Opened in 2014, it was purpose-built for art and luxury goods with advanced climate control and security. Luxembourg now requires trustees storing art inside the freeport to disclose the identity of trust beneficiaries, a rule that sets it apart from less transparent jurisdictions.

**Singapore Freeport.** Launched in 2010, it serves the growing Asian collector market. Singapore's position as a fast-growing art hub (art imports surged 74% in 2024 to nearly $1.7 billion, per the Art Basel/UBS report) makes it an increasingly important storage location for buyers across Asia.

**Delaware, United States.** Delaware functions like a freeport for American collectors because it charges no state sales tax on art. While it lacks the bonded warehouse structure of European freeports, the tax treatment achieves a similar result for domestic transactions. Combined with the fact that the United States charges no federal import duty on fine art, Delaware storage offers meaningful tax savings for U.S. buyers.

## What Happens to Market Transparency

Here is where freeports matter most for investors, and where the picture gets less comfortable.

Art that sits in a freeport is, in a real sense, invisible to the market. It does not appear in auction records. It does not generate public transaction data. It does not get photographed for catalogues or shown in galleries where other collectors can see it. When a work changes hands inside a freeport, the sale happens privately, at a price agreed between two parties, with no public record.

This matters for investors because price discovery in the art market already depends on a thin set of data points, mainly public auction results. When a meaningful share of the world's most valuable art sits in freeports and trades privately within them, the available data becomes even thinner. The prices you see at auction may not reflect the full picture of what comparable works are actually trading for.

The scale of this blind spot is hard to pin down, precisely because freeport transactions are not reported. But given that the Geneva Freeport alone holds an estimated $100 billion in goods, and that private sales now make up a growing share of the overall art market (the Art Basel/UBS report pegs private dealer sales at $39.8 billion in 2025), the volume of art sitting outside public price discovery is substantial.

For investors trying to assess whether a work is fairly valued, this opacity is a real limitation. Auction comps may understate or overstate true market clearing prices if a large share of comparable works are trading quietly inside vaults.

## Regulatory Pressure Is Building

Freeports have operated with light oversight for decades, but that is changing. Several forces are tightening the rules.

**EU import regulation 2019/880**, which came into force in June 2025, now requires all cultural goods to show proof of legal export from their country of origin before being imported into any EU member state. This has pushed some collectors to look at non-EU storage options like Geneva and Singapore.

**Switzerland has capped storage duration.** An amendment to the Swiss Customs Act now limits freeport storage to six months for certain categories of goods, though art has largely been carved out or handled through renewals. The signal, however, is clear: Swiss regulators are less comfortable with open-ended storage than they once were.

**The European Parliament** has called for mandatory ownership disclosures, transaction tracking, and regular audits of assets within freeports. These proposals are still working through the legislative process, but the direction of travel points toward more transparency and higher compliance costs.

**The IRS in the United States** has stepped up its review of art donations involving works stored in freeports. Collectors who donate freeport-stored art to museums and claim fair-market-value tax deductions now face closer scrutiny of the valuations used, especially when the art has not been publicly exhibited or recently sold.

**Money laundering risk** remains the sharpest criticism. Freeports allow ownership to be held through shell companies, transactions to happen privately, and assets to move without public record. The OECD flagged free trade zones, including freeports, as high-risk for money laundering as early as 2010, and the European Parliament has called freeports potential "havens for dirty money." The Geneva Freeport was at the center of the "Bouvier Affair," in which dealer Yves Bouvier allegedly overcharged Russian oligarch Dmitry Rybolovlev on works traded without ever leaving the facility.

None of this means freeports are going away. The tax savings and logistical benefits are too large, and the global collector class is too mobile. But the compliance burden is rising, and investors should assume that the regulatory environment will be stricter in five years than it is today.

## What This Means for Art Investors

For most art investors, freeports are not something you will use directly. But they shape the market you invest in.

**They affect supply.** Art locked in freeport storage is art that is not available on the open market. If thousands of high-value works by blue-chip artists sit in Geneva vaults, the supply of comparable pieces at auction is lower than it would otherwise be, which supports prices.

**They affect price data.** Private freeport transactions do not feed into the auction records and indices that investors rely on. This means market data may not capture the full range of prices at which works are actually trading, making valuations harder to verify.

**They affect regulatory risk.** If new rules force greater transparency or limit tax advantages, collectors who currently store art in freeports may begin selling, increasing supply. Or they may move art to less regulated jurisdictions, shifting the geography of the market. Either way, regulatory changes to freeport rules could ripple through pricing in ways that are hard to predict.

**They affect trust.** The association between freeports and money laundering, tax avoidance, and opacity is a headwind for the art market's broader push toward institutional credibility. As art seeks acceptance as a legitimate asset class alongside private equity and real estate, the freeport system's lack of transparency cuts against that effort.

## The Bottom Line

- Art freeports allow collectors to store, buy, and sell artwork without paying import duties, VAT, or capital gains taxes, for as long as the work stays inside the facility.
- The Geneva Freeport alone holds an estimated $100 billion in goods, with roughly 40% consisting of art, including an estimated 1,000 Picassos.
- Freeports reduce market transparency by keeping high-value transactions private and outside public auction records, making price discovery harder for all investors.
- Regulatory pressure is rising: the EU now requires proof of legal export for cultural goods, Switzerland has moved to cap storage durations, and the European Parliament has called for mandatory ownership disclosures.
- For art investors, freeports matter because they affect supply, price data, regulatory risk, and the broader credibility of art as an asset class.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to store art in a freeport?
Monthly storage fees at major freeports are broadly in line with high-end art storage elsewhere, typically ranging from a few hundred to a few thousand dollars per month depending on the size and value of the work. The real savings come from tax deferral, not cheap rent. On a $10 million painting, avoiding 10% to 20% VAT dwarfs the annual storage bill.

### Can art be sold inside a freeport without any taxes?
Yes. Art can change hands multiple times inside a freeport without triggering import duties, VAT, or capital gains taxes, because the work is legally treated as "in transit" and has not entered any country's tax jurisdiction. Taxes apply only when the art physically leaves the freeport and clears customs into a taxable territory.

### Are freeports legal?
Freeports are fully legal and operate under customs regulations in their host countries. The tax deferral they offer is a feature of customs law, not a loophole. However, the privacy and opacity they provide have drawn criticism from regulators concerned about money laundering and tax evasion, and rules are tightening across Europe and the United States.

### What happens if regulations force freeports to close or change?
If new rules reduce the tax benefits or require greater transparency, some collectors may move art to less regulated jurisdictions, while others may sell holdings they no longer see a tax benefit in storing. Either outcome could affect supply and pricing in the open market. The most likely near-term change is increased disclosure requirements rather than outright closure.
