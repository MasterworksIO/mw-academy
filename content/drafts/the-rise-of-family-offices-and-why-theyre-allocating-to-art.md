Family offices now manage more than $3.1 trillion in assets worldwide, and a growing share of that capital is flowing into art. According to the 2024 Deloitte Art & Finance Report, family offices allocate an average of 13.4% of their portfolios to art and collectibles, well above the 8.6% average among private banks. That gap tells a story about how the wealthiest families think about art: less as decoration, more as a distinct asset class sitting alongside private equity, real estate, and hedge funds.

This piece looks at the numbers behind the family office boom, how these institutions approach art differently from individual collectors, and what their behavior signals for the broader art investment market.

## The Family Office Boom in Numbers

The global count of single family offices has climbed to roughly 8,030 as of 2025, up from about 6,130 just a few years earlier, according to Deloitte Private. By 2030, that number is expected to pass 10,700. North America accounts for the largest share, with around 3,180 offices, followed by Asia Pacific at 2,290 and Europe at 2,020. The Middle East, South America, and Africa make up the rest.

Total assets under management across these offices sit at $3.1 trillion today, with projections pointing to $5.4 trillion by 2030, a 73% jump. The average single family office in the UBS Global Family Office Report 2025 manages $1.1 billion, with the families behind them averaging $2.7 billion in net worth.

What drives this growth is straightforward: more wealth, more complexity. As fortunes grow and spread across asset classes, geographies, and generations, the single family office becomes the default structure for managing it all under one roof. And as these offices get bigger and more professional, they start behaving more like institutional investors, complete with CIOs, investment committees, and formal allocation targets.

## Where Art Sits in the Alternatives Stack

Family offices are heavy allocators to alternatives. The UBS 2025 report pegs the average split at 56% traditional assets and 44% alternatives. BlackRock's 2025 survey of 175 single family offices found a similar figure: 42% in alternatives, up from 39% in the prior year. Within that bucket, private equity leads at roughly 21% to 25%, followed by real estate at 11% to 18%, hedge funds at 4%, and private credit at 4%.

Art and antiques, in the UBS data, account for about 1% of the average family office portfolio. That number looks small in isolation. But it masks wide variation: some offices hold zero art, while others, particularly in Europe and Asia, hold far more. The Deloitte figure of 13.4% comes from a survey of offices that actively engage with art and collectibles, which skews the number upward. The truth is somewhere in between, and the direction is clearly toward more.

What matters is not the precise percentage but the trend. BlackRock notes that niche investments like collectibles, farmland, and structured litigation finance are gaining traction as families look for uncorrelated sources of return. Art fits that profile. Its price movements have historically shown little correlation to equities or bonds. During the early 2020 sell-off, art prices did not crash in step with stocks. During the 2021 and 2022 boom, high-end art surged even as bond yields stayed low.

## How Family Offices Approach Art Differently

An individual collector buys what they like. A family office buys what makes sense for the balance sheet, the tax plan, the estate structure, and the next generation.

That distinction shapes everything. Family offices bring institutional discipline to art: formal due diligence on provenance (the documented history of who has owned a work), independent appraisals, insurance reviews, and storage logistics. Many work with dedicated art advisors or firms like the Fine Art Group rather than relying on gallery relationships alone. UBS runs an in-house art advisory practice specifically for this market.

The governance piece is real, too. Sixty-five percent of North American family offices now operate with a family charter or constitution, according to industry surveys. Art collections, when they grow large enough, get folded into these governance structures. That means committee oversight on purchases, formal collection policies, and succession plans for the art itself.

Private transactions hold special appeal for family offices. Discretion matters to families who do not want their buying activity publicized. Working through advisors and private dealers gives access to works that never appear at auction, and it avoids the buyer's premium (the fee charged on top of the hammer price, typically 20% to 26% at major auction houses).

Legacy is the other dimension. A collection can carry a family's identity across generations. Structured correctly, artworks can be placed into trusts, foundations, or lending arrangements with museums, creating tax advantages while keeping the collection intact. The art becomes both a financial asset and a cultural one.

## The $992 Billion Wealth Transfer

A massive wave of art is about to change hands. The Deloitte 2025 Art & Finance Report estimates that $992 billion in art and collectibles will transfer between generations over the next decade, roughly $100 billion per year. This tracks with the broader wealth transfer: up to 1.2 million individuals with a net worth above $5 million are expected to pass along nearly $31 trillion in total.

For family offices, this transfer creates both a challenge and an opportunity. On the challenge side, inheriting a collection worth tens or hundreds of millions of dollars requires expertise that many next-generation family members do not have. The Deloitte report found that client requests for art-related support from wealth managers have surged to 65%, up from 44% just two years earlier. Collection management, estate planning, and valuation services are all in higher demand.

On the opportunity side, this transfer puts enormous amounts of art onto the market. Some heirs will keep what they inherit. Others will sell. Family offices that are positioned to buy, with advisors, capital, and storage in place, can pick up works at private sale before they ever reach an auction house.

Art and collectible wealth among ultra-high-net-worth individuals grew from $2.17 trillion in 2022 to $2.56 trillion in 2024, an 18% rise. Projections put the figure at $3.47 trillion by 2030. The pool of investable art is getting larger, and family offices are the natural buyers.

## The Market Case for Art in a Portfolio

The investment argument for art rests on three pillars: low correlation, long-term appreciation, and inflation sensitivity.

On correlation, art prices have historically moved independently of stocks and bonds. This makes art useful as a diversifier, particularly in portfolios already heavy in equities and private equity. During periods of equity volatility, art has tended to hold value or even appreciate.

On returns, the numbers are mixed but directionally positive for high-quality works. The Artprice100 index, which tracks the 100 top-performing artists at auction, rose roughly 433% from 2000 through 2023, outpacing the S&P 500 over the same period. But these figures carry survivorship bias. Not all art appreciates, and the index tracks winners by definition.

The 2024 global art market tells a more nuanced story. Total sales fell 12% to $57.5 billion, driven largely by a 45% contraction in the $10 million-plus segment. But volume actually rose 3%, and the mid-market (works priced between $50,000 and $1 million) generated around $8 billion in auction sales. Post-war and contemporary art remains the most traded category, producing $1.8 billion in auction revenue in the first half of 2025 alone, per Artnet.

For family offices, the mid-market is often where the real action happens. Trophy pieces at $50 million or more grab headlines, but works in the $100,000 to $5 million range offer stronger liquidity and more consistent price support. The data backs this up: mid-market segments contracted at slower rates than the ultra-high end during the 2024 correction.

## The Gaps That Remain

For all the growth in family office art activity, the infrastructure is still catching up. The Deloitte 2025 report found that the share of wealth managers offering dedicated art services actually fell from 63% in 2023 to 51% in 2025. That means nearly half of all wealth management firms still do not offer art-related support, even as client demand for it rises.

Family offices are filling this gap with hybrid models, partnering with external art advisors, auction houses, and specialized firms rather than building art expertise in-house. Twenty-two percent of family offices see strong appetite for art investment services like art funds, managed accounts, and fractional investment platforms.

Liquidity remains the biggest structural limitation. Art is illiquid by nature. Selling a major work can take months, and the outcome depends on timing, market conditions, and the specific tastes of a small pool of buyers. This is why most family offices treat art as a long-term hold, measured in decades rather than quarters.

Valuation is another challenge. Unlike stocks or bonds, art has no ticker price. Appraisals are subjective, and two qualified appraisers can reach meaningfully different conclusions about the same work. For family offices that need to report asset values to beneficiaries or comply with regulatory requirements, this ambiguity adds complexity.

## The Bottom Line

- Family offices manage over $3.1 trillion globally and allocate 44% of their portfolios to alternatives, with art and collectibles forming a small but growing slice of that allocation.
- The Deloitte Art & Finance Report puts the average family office art allocation at 13.4% among those that actively engage with the asset class, far above the private bank average of 8.6%.
- A $992 billion wave of art and collectibles is set to change hands over the next decade as wealth transfers between generations, creating both buying opportunities and demand for advisory services.
- Family offices approach art with institutional rigor: formal governance, professional advisors, private transactions, and integration into estate and tax planning.
- Art's low correlation to stocks and bonds makes it useful for portfolio diversification, but illiquidity and subjective valuation remain real constraints that limit how much capital most offices will commit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much do family offices typically allocate to art?

Allocations vary widely. The UBS Global Family Office Report 2025 shows art and antiques at about 1% of the average family office portfolio across all respondents. Among offices that actively invest in art, the Deloitte Art & Finance Report found an average allocation of 13.4%. The gap reflects the fact that art investment is concentrated among a subset of family offices, particularly those in Europe and Asia with multigenerational collections.

### Why do family offices prefer private art transactions over auctions?

Privacy is the main driver. Family offices often do not want their buying or selling activity to become public information, which auction results inevitably are. Private sales also avoid buyer's premiums, which can add 20% to 26% to the purchase price at major auction houses. Working through advisors and private dealers also gives access to works that may never reach the public market.

### How does art compare to other alternative investments for family offices?

Art offers low correlation to equities and bonds, which makes it useful for diversification. But it lacks the cash flow of real estate, the defined return profiles of private credit, and the liquidity of hedge funds. Most family offices view art as a complement to their broader alternatives allocation rather than a replacement for any single asset class. The investment horizon is typically 10 years or longer.

### What role does the generational wealth transfer play in family office art strategy?

The Deloitte 2025 report estimates $992 billion in art and collectibles will transfer between generations over the next decade. This creates two dynamics: family offices with existing collections need succession plans and professional management for inherited works, and offices with capital to deploy will find buying opportunities as some heirs sell inherited pieces. Demand for collection management and art estate planning services has risen sharply as a result.
