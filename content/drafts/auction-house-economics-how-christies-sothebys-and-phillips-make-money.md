Christie's, Sotheby's, and Phillips earn money by charging fees on both sides of every sale: a buyer's premium added on top of the hammer price and a seller's commission taken from the consignor's proceeds. On a mid-market lot, the house's gross take typically runs 25% to 35% of the hammer price. But the fee line is only part of the story. The Big Three now pull billions more from private sales, art-backed lending, luxury goods, and the high-stakes business of guaranteeing outcomes before the gavel falls. For art investors, knowing where the money flows matters because these incentives shape which works get sold, how they are priced, and who bears the risk when a market turns.

## The Buyer's Premium: Where Most Revenue Starts

Every winning bidder at a major auction pays more than the hammer price. The extra charge, called the buyer's premium, is the single biggest source of auction house income.

The rates are tiered. At Christie's, following a September 2025 increase, buyers pay 27% on the first $1.5 million of hammer price, 22% on the portion between $1.5 million and $8 million, and 15% above that. Sotheby's matches Christie's almost exactly after reverting its fee structure in early 2025: 27% up to $1 million, 22% from $1 million to $8 million, and 15% beyond. Phillips stands out with the steepest base rate at 29% for lots under $1 million, though it offers a "priority bid" discount (25% instead of 29%) to collectors who place written bids at least 48 hours before a sale.

The math adds up fast. On a painting that hammers at $1 million, the buyer at Christie's or Sotheby's pays $270,000 in premium alone. At Phillips without the early-bird discount, the same hammer price costs $290,000 extra. For a $10 million lot, the blended premium at Christie's comes to roughly $1.7 million.

These rates have crept upward for decades. Christie's raised its premium three times in under three years leading into 2025. The trend is clear: as competition for top consignments drives seller commissions down, the houses shift costs onto buyers.

## Seller Commissions: The Negotiable Side

Consignors pay their own fee, but the rate is far less predictable. While buyer premiums are published, seller commissions are negotiated deal by deal, and the bigger the lot, the smaller the cut.

For everyday material, a seller might pay 10% to 15% of the hammer price. But for trophy lots worth tens of millions, the commission can shrink to zero. Houses will waive the seller's fee entirely to win a headline consignment, banking instead on the buyer's premium and the publicity the sale brings.

Sotheby's tried to reshape this model in early 2024, rolling out a flat 10% seller's commission for works estimated up to $5 million paired with a slashed buyer's premium. The idea was to make pricing clearer. It backfired. Sellers found the structure less attractive, and by late 2024 Sotheby's reversed course, returning to tiered buyer's premiums and flexible seller terms.

The lesson for investors: published fee schedules tell you what buyers pay but only hint at what sellers keep. Two identical works sold at the same house on the same night can carry very different commission arrangements.

## Third-Party Guarantees: Selling Before the Sale

Guarantees have become the most talked-about financial tool in the auction business. A guarantee is a minimum price promised to the seller before a lot goes to auction. If bidding fails to reach that floor, the house (or a third party) buys the work at the guaranteed amount.

There are two kinds. A house guarantee means the auction house puts its own money on the line. A third-party guarantee (also called an irrevocable bid) shifts that risk to an outside backer, often a dealer, collector, or fund. The backer agrees in writing to buy the lot at a set price if no one else bids higher. In return, the guarantor gets a financing fee or a cut of the buyer's premium, typically if the lot sells to someone else. If bidding exceeds the guaranteed price, the guarantor may also receive 15% to 20% of the upside, according to industry insiders.

Guarantees serve clear purposes. Sellers get certainty. The house locks down marquee consignments. And the guarantor gets a risk-adjusted return with an informational edge: they know the reserve price, the lowest level the seller will accept.

That edge is also the core problem. Guarantors know the reserve. They often know the auction house staff personally because most are also clients. Critics argue this creates a two-tier market where connected insiders bid with better information than everyone else in the room. Some observers note that heavily guaranteed sales can lose competitive energy, with other bidders sensing that lots are already spoken for.

The financial risks are real. In autumn 2008, Sotheby's reportedly lost $52 million in a single season from house guarantees that went wrong. The houses have since shifted most guarantee risk to third parties, but the practice remains a flashpoint for anyone concerned about price transparency in the art market.

## Private Sales: The Quiet Revenue Engine

Not everything goes through the saleroom. Private sales, where the house brokers a deal between buyer and seller without a public auction, have grown into a massive business line.

Christie's recorded roughly $1.5 billion in private sales during 2025, up from about $800 million in 2019. Sotheby's private sales hit $1.2 billion in 2025. Together, private transactions now account for 15% to 20% of each house's total volume.

The appeal for the houses is straightforward: private sales carry fewer overhead costs than staging a live auction, and the commission rate can be higher because sellers value discretion. For sellers, a private deal avoids the public risk of a work failing to sell (a "buy-in" in auction language), which can damage an artist's market.

For investors tracking the art market, the growth of private sales matters because these transactions are opaque. They do not appear in public auction records, which means the price indices and market data that many investors rely on capture only part of the picture.

## Luxury Goods: Plugging the Gap

Fine art still anchors the business, but luxury goods have become a critical growth category. Handbags, watches, wine, jewelry, and collectible cars now fill a roughly $3 billion hole left by softening high-end art sales in recent years, according to ARTnews reporting.

Christie's luxury division topped $1 billion in global sales during 2025 (excluding private sales), with a 90% sell-through rate. Luxury goods made up 22% of Christie's first-half 2025 total. At Sotheby's, luxury turnover rose 22% year-on-year to $2.7 billion.

The shift has strategic value beyond revenue. Christie's reports that luxury categories recruited 41% of its new buyers, many of whom later buy across other categories including fine art. Lower price points on watches and handbags draw younger collectors who may grow into serious art buyers.

## Financial Services: Lending Against Art

Sotheby's Financial Services runs what amounts to a specialty lending operation. Collectors borrow against the appraised value of their art, typically at loan-to-value ratios of 50% to 60%, without selling the work.

The division hit a record portfolio balance above $1.8 billion in 2025. In January 2026, Sotheby's completed a $900 million securitization, packaging art-secured and (for the first time) collectible-car-secured loans into notes sold to institutional investors. The transaction was significantly oversubscribed, signaling strong appetite from bond markets for art-backed debt.

Over its five-year partnership with Alexander Klabin and Ancient, Sotheby's Financial Services grew its portfolio by more than $1 billion and tripled its profitability.

For art investors, this matters in two ways. First, lending generates steady fee income that is far less volatile than auction commissions, giving the house a financial cushion during market downturns. Second, the growth of art-backed lending expands the pool of capital treating art as a financial asset, which over time supports price levels across the market.

## 2025 Results: Recovery, but Uneven

The Big Three posted a combined $14.1 billion in sales for 2025, up roughly 10% from 2024.

Sotheby's led with $7.1 billion in consolidated sales (up 18%) and $1.4 billion in revenue (up 21%). Fine art rose 15% to $4.3 billion. But those top-line numbers mask a painful balance sheet: Sotheby's reported a $248 million pretax loss for fiscal 2024, more than double the prior year, driven by falling commission income, rising severance costs ($29.2 million, up from $11.4 million), and the debt load from Patrick Drahi's 2019 leveraged buyout. An August 2024 capital injection from Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund ADQ, worth $909 million for a 24% stake, gave the company breathing room.

Christie's posted $6.2 billion in total sales (up 6%), with public auctions at $4.7 billion (up 8%) and luxury goods at $795 million (up 17%).

Phillips reported $927 million in total sales (up 10%), holding its position as the smallest of the three but growing at a competitive clip.

The recovery is real but fragile. Asian sales still lag, and the market remains concentrated in trophy lots while the mid-market stays soft. Revenue growth has come partly from raising fees and partly from luxury goods, not from a broad rebound in art buying.

## What This Means for Art Investors

Auction house fee structures directly affect net returns on art. A buyer paying 27% premium on a $500,000 purchase starts with a $135,000 cost basis above hammer price. To break even after selling (and paying a seller's commission), the work needs to appreciate meaningfully just to cover transaction costs.

The guarantee system shapes pricing in ways that can benefit or hurt investors. A guarantee sets a floor, which can support an artist's market. But it can also inflate sale prices if the guarantor's financing fee gets baked into the final number, creating a misleading signal in auction records.

The growth of private sales, meanwhile, means that the public auction data investors use for valuation captures a shrinking share of total transactions. Artnet, Art Basel, and other market reports acknowledge this gap but cannot fully close it.

And the houses' push into lending and luxury goods shows where the industry sees its future: less dependent on the unpredictable cycle of blockbuster consignments, more focused on recurring fee income and a broader collector base. For anyone investing in art through any channel, these structural shifts in how the auction houses operate are worth watching closely.

## The Bottom Line

- Christie's, Sotheby's, and Phillips earn 25% to 35% of hammer price on mid-market lots through combined buyer premiums and seller commissions, though the effective rate drops below 15% on trophy works.
- Third-party guarantees have shifted sale risk from houses to outside backers, but they create information gaps that benefit guarantors over regular bidders.
- Private sales now account for $1.2 billion to $1.5 billion per house annually, and these transactions do not appear in public auction records that investors use for pricing.
- Luxury goods (watches, handbags, jewelry, wine) contributed over $3.5 billion across the Big Three in 2025, recruiting new buyers and offsetting softness in high-end art.
- Sotheby's Financial Services holds a record $1.8 billion art-lending portfolio, and the securitization of art-backed loans signals growing institutional acceptance of art as collateral.
- Transaction costs matter: at current premium levels, a $500,000 purchase carries $135,000 in buyer's premium alone before any seller-side fees on exit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a buyer actually pay above the hammer price at auction?
At Christie's and Sotheby's, buyers pay a 27% premium on the first $1 million to $1.5 million of hammer price, stepping down to 22% and then 15% at higher tiers. On a $1 million lot, that means roughly $270,000 in added cost. Phillips charges up to 29% at the base tier, though early bidders can qualify for a reduced 25% rate.

### What is a third-party guarantee at auction?
A third-party guarantee (also called an irrevocable bid) is a binding commitment from an outside party to buy a lot at a set minimum price if no other bidder meets that level. The guarantor receives a fee or a share of upside if the lot sells above the guarantee. This shifts financial risk away from the auction house and gives the seller a price floor, but it also gives the guarantor advance knowledge of the reserve price.

### Do auction houses make money when a lot fails to sell?
Generally, no. When a lot does not sell (called a "buy-in"), neither the buyer's premium nor the seller's commission applies. However, if the house issued a guarantee on that lot, it must buy the work itself at the guaranteed price, turning a missed sale into a forced purchase. This is why most guarantees are now backed by third parties rather than house balance sheets.

### How do private art sales differ from auction sales for investors?
Private sales happen off the public record. The price, the buyer, and the seller all stay confidential. This means private sales do not feed into the auction databases that underpin art market indices and valuation tools. For investors, this creates a data gap: the publicly available price history for an artist may reflect only a portion of actual market activity.

### Why are auction houses selling luxury goods like handbags and watches?
Luxury categories bring in younger buyers at lower price points, generate strong margins, and provide revenue that is less dependent on the cyclical high-end art market. Christie's reported that 41% of its new buyers in 2025 came through luxury categories, and many of those buyers later crossed into fine art. For the houses, luxury goods function as both a growth engine and a client pipeline.
