## Instagram

### Caption
Asian collectors bid 40% higher than other buyers on artworks priced above $1 million. That is not a typo.

Between 2018 and 2022, Sotheby's data shows Asian bidders on works above $1M spent 20% more on average and placed bids 40% higher than other buyers. In 2024, Asia-Pacific buyers made up 26% of Christie's global auction turnover.

The global art market reached $59.6 billion in 2025. And the map is changing fast. Singapore's art imports surged 74% to nearly $1.7 billion. Japan's imports doubled to just over $1.1 billion. These are not small shifts.

But this is not a one-way story. Prices in mainland China and Hong Kong fell 38% in 2024, according to ArtTactic. Asian collectors have grown more selective, focusing on proven names like Picasso, Monet, and Basquiat while pulling back from mid-tier works.

For your portfolio, the lesson is clear. Geographic spread of collector demand matters. An artwork with deep interest from American, European, and Asian buyers is a structurally different asset than one whose recent price gains came from a single region.

## LinkedIn

### Post
Asian collectors placed bids 40% higher than other buyers on artworks above $1 million between 2018 and 2022. The pricing effect is structural, not anecdotal.

In 2024, Asia-Pacific buyers made up 26% of Christie's global auction turnover, spending heavily on Van Gogh and Monet works that each topped $25.6 million at Hong Kong sales. At Sotheby's New York in November 2025, the dollar value of Asian bids doubled year over year to its highest level in five years.

But the data tells a more complicated story than "Asia is buying everything." Prices in mainland China and Hong Kong fell 38% in 2024, per ArtTactic. Asian share at Christie's dipped from 26% to 23% in 2025. The mid-tier ($200,000 to $500,000) has cooled the most, while blue-chip names like Picasso and Basquiat hold firm.

What is growing fast: new demand centers. Singapore's art imports surged 74% to nearly $1.7 billion. Japan's imports doubled. At Christie's Asia in 2025, 44% of bidders were millennials or Gen-Z.
