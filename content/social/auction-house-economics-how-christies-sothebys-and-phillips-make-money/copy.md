### Caption
Win a $1 million lot at auction. You just spent $1.27 million.

That extra $270,000? It's the buyer's premium, the single biggest source of income for Christie's and Sotheby's. And the rates keep climbing. Christie's raised its premium three times in under three years leading into 2025.

But auction fees are only the start. The Big Three pulled in $14.1 billion in combined sales last year. Here's where the rest comes from:

Private sales hit $1.5 billion at Christie's alone, up from $800 million in 2019. These deals never show up in public auction records.

Luxury goods, from watches to handbags, now account for over $3.5 billion across the three houses. Christie's says 41% of its new buyers entered through luxury categories.

Sotheby's runs a $1.8 billion art-lending portfolio, and in January 2026 it packaged art-backed loans into securities sold to institutional investors.

For anyone investing in artworks, these fee structures and hidden revenue streams affect your net returns at every stage.

## LinkedIn

### Post
A $500,000 artwork purchase carries $135,000 in buyer's premium before a single dollar of appreciation.

The Big Three auction houses, Christie's, Sotheby's, and Phillips, posted $14.1 billion in combined 2025 sales. The numbers look like a recovery. But where the money actually comes from has shifted in ways most investors overlook.

Buyer's premiums now run 27% to 29% at the base tier. Christie's raised its rates three times in under three years. The pattern is consistent: as houses compete for top consignments by cutting seller commissions, the cost shifts to buyers.

Private sales have nearly doubled at Christie's since 2019, reaching $1.5 billion. These transactions are invisible to the auction databases that underpin most art market indices. If you rely on public price data, you are working with an incomplete picture.

Meanwhile, luxury goods contributed over $3.5 billion across the Big Three in 2025, and Sotheby's Financial Services holds a record $1.8 billion art-lending portfolio. The houses are building recurring fee income that is less dependent on blockbuster consignments.
