# Social Copy: Dollar Strength and Art Prices: Why Currency Matters at Auction

## Instagram

### Caption

The US dollar fell roughly 10% in the first half of 2025. That was its steepest decline since 1973, according to EBC Financial Group.

For anyone watching the art market, the effects were immediate.

European collectors got an effective 12% to 18% discount on artworks priced in US dollars. Christie's Americas auction sales surged 15% year over year, while EMEA grew just 2% and Asia fell 5%. Foreign capital poured into New York sale rooms.

But here is the part most people miss: art returns are always realized in a currency. An American investor who bought in London and sold in New York carries implicit GBP/USD exposure. A portfolio that returned 8% in a year when the dollar fell 10% against the euro actually lost ground for a euro-based investor.

Global art sales reached $59.6 billion in 2025 (Art Basel/UBS 2026). That headline number, reported in dollars, overstates growth in local-currency terms for many markets outside the US.

Masterworks structures art investments for US-based investors in dollars, which simplifies the currency question. But the underlying artworks still trade in a global market where exchange rates shape effective returns.

Currency is a risk factor that often goes unpriced in art. Your portfolio should account for it.

## LinkedIn

### Post

The US dollar fell roughly 10% in the first half of 2025, its steepest decline since 1973.

Most art market commentary focused on what sold and for how much. Almost nobody talked about the currency layer underneath.

Here is what the data shows: Christie's Americas auction sales surged 15% year over year, while EMEA managed just 2% and Asia fell 5%. The weak dollar drew foreign capital into New York, creating an effective 12% to 18% purchasing gap for European and Asian collectors bidding in US sale rooms (MyArtBroker, Nov 2025).

Global art sales reached $59.6 billion in 2025, up 4% year over year, according to the Art Basel and UBS Global Art Market Report 2026. But that dollar-denominated headline overstates growth in local-currency terms. Markets that grew modestly in euros or sterling looked stronger when converted back to a weakened dollar.

For investors holding art as a financial asset, this matters more than most realize. Art returns are always realized in a currency. A portfolio that returned 8% in a year when the dollar fell 10% against the euro actually lost ground for a euro-based investor. Most art market indices report in US dollars and embed exchange-rate effects without disclosing them.

Masterworks denominates returns in dollars for US-based investors, which simplifies the currency question. But the underlying artworks still trade globally, and implicit FX exposure comes with every cross-border transaction.

Currency risk in art is real, measurable, and almost never discussed. What are you seeing in your own cross-border transactions?
