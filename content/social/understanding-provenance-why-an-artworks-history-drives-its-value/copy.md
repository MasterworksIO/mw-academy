### Post

Provenance adds up to 54% to an artwork's hammer price. But only 2% of auctioned artworks come with a certificate of authentication.

A 2022 study in European Financial Management analyzed nearly 3 million auction transactions and broke provenance into four price signals. Literature references (published in catalogues raisonne or art books) commanded the highest premium at 54%. Exhibition history added 42%. Pedigree records, the documented chain of prior owners, added 21%. Certificates added 14%.

The scarcity of documentation is part of what drives the premium. Only 14% of auctioned artworks carried pedigree records. Just 5% had exhibition histories or literature references.

Celebrity provenance amplifies the effect further. The Paul Allen collection at Christie's in November 2022 brought $1.5 billion across 60 lots with a 100% sell-through rate. Single-owner sales now account for roughly a third of major auction house volume.

On the risk side, provenance gaps between 1933 and 1945 create real financial exposure. New arbitration mechanisms in Germany (October 2024) and restitution laws in France (February 2024) are making claims easier to file, not harder. Even the shadow of a claim can reduce a work's market value by 10% to 30%.
